Scarcity is one of those concepts that sounds obvious until you start seeing it everywhere.

## The basic idea

Resources are finite. Time, money, attention, energy — there's never enough of everything. This forces trade-offs. Every yes is a no to something else. Every dollar spent on one thing is a dollar not spent on another.

This isn't just economics. It's a fundamental constraint on how people make decisions.

## Scarcity in product decisions

When you're building a product, scarcity shows up as prioritization. You can't build every feature. You can't serve every market. You can't fix every bug. The teams that ship well are the ones that accept this early and make deliberate choices about what to cut.

The hard part isn't having ideas — it's choosing which ones to say no to. Scarcity forces that choice.

## Artificial scarcity

Businesses create scarcity intentionally. Limited drops, exclusive access, time-limited offers — these aren't accidents. They're leveraging the psychology of scarcity to drive urgency.

This works because scarcity changes how we value things. A thing that's scarce feels more valuable, even when the underlying utility hasn't changed. It's irrational but consistent.

## The attention economy

The most relevant form of scarcity today is attention. There are more apps, more content, more notifications than anyone can process. Products compete for a finite resource — the user's focus.

This reframes product design. The question isn't "what can we add?" but "what's worth someone's limited attention?" Products that respect this constraint tend to win.

Scarcity isn't a problem to solve. It's a reality to design around.
